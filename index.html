Publications

Lin Jing, Roland Cheo
April 2013 Ecnomics Letters

House money effects, risk preferences and the public goods game

This paper investigates whether risk preferences inform the decision of how much to put into the public account in the public goods game under the three different frames (the standard and covered-loss frames, as well as the real-loss frame). The main contribution of this paper finds that the covered loss and real loss treatments are statistically equivalent. This assures researchers that just introducing the notion of loss into an experimental treatment without the need for participants to realize a real loss is still a valid experimental instrument. We also find that the house money effect is a better explanation for the difference in contributions between gain and loss framing than loss aversion.

Working Papers

Lin Jing, Yohanes Eko Riyanto
November 2021 (Jor Market Paper)

Certainty and Severity of Punishment in Crime and Corruption Deterrence: An Experimental Study

We investigate experimentally the (relative) effectiveness of certainty and severity of punishment in deterring crime and corruption simultaneously in a game developed by Ortner & Chassang(2018). The experimental design features two different policy regimes, HP with a high certainty and low severity of punishment and LP the other way around. Within each regime, we examine whether there is a real deterrent effect by increasing the certainty or severity of punishment, and which one delivers a greater impact if there is any. We show that, in the regime LP, neither increasing certainty nor increasing the severity of punishment deters crime or corruption effectively. In contrast, both increasing the certainty and severity of punishment significantly deter crime and corruption in regime HP where the certainty of punishment is high enough, and an increase in the certainty delivers a greater deterrent effect than that in the severity. In addition, we document the presence of the Cobra Effect in the regime LP when we intend to deter corruption by increasing the expected wage of the monitor. Last but not least, we explore the changes in extensive and intensive margins of crime and corruption, and we find a difference in celerity of deterrent effect between increasing the certainty and severity of punishment in regime HP, specifically, subjects are promptly responsive to changes in the certainty of punishment while they are inertial to changes in the severity of punishment.

Lin Jing, Yohanes Eko Riyanto
August 2021

Selection of Leadership: Effectiveness in Promoting Cooperation and Coordination under Asymmetric Information

Leadership has been considered as one important instrument that affects both the qualitative and quantitative level of coordination and cooperation in group projects. Using a threshold public goods game, we experimentally investigate how leaders (as well as how they are selected) affect cooperation and coordination by sharing her privately observed information instead of by leading-by-example. The experimental results show that the presence of the leader does not have a significant effect when she is randomly selected. However, when the leader is elected by her group members, the contributions as well as the public goods provided are significantly higher, and the welfare also increases significantly. Via a Diff-in-Diff approach, we also show that the groups with elected leaders perform significantly better than those groups without a leader or without randomly selected leaders. Nonetheless, there is not a significant difference between groups with elected leaders and groups with nominated leaders by ability or social preference. Last but not least, we find that, when the leader is elected by their group members, the leader tends to be less manipulative against her members, and the group members tend to be more compliant to coordinate at the message sent by the leader.
Lin Jing, Yohanes Eko Riyanto
July 2020

The Role of Information Structure on Cooperation and Coordination: An Experiment

We economists normally think that an environment with complete and thus symmetric (CS) information would deliver the best economic outcome since each agent can fully optimize without paying any informational rent to anyone else. However, in a simple threshold public goods game with three different information structures about the threshold value (CS, Complete and Symmetric; IS, Incomplete and Symmetric; IA, Incomplete and Asymmetric), the theory prediction suggests that the CS information structure actually performs the worst, while the IS information structure performs the best, which is counterintuitive. We then experimentally investigate how people would actually behave under those different information structures, and the result shows, a bit surprisingly, that the IA information structure (with cheap talk) leads to the highest contribution and highest provision of public goods. The reason is twofold, the subjects are less manipulative when they are privately informed, and they are more willing to coordinate at the suggested level when they are not privately informed. This suggests that, in order to achieve economic optimality, we can cultivate a certain set of attributes to jointly deliver the best outcome under information asymmetry, rather than trying to eliminate information asymmetry completely. We also investigate how the change in MPCR would interact with these three information structures, and our main results are robust against MPCR changes.

Works in Progress

Lin Jing, Jubo Yan
Oct 7, 2020

Information Structure, Endogenous Goal Setting, and Productivity

A large extant literature has examined behavioral interventions that can enhance productivity. Goal setting are among the most extensively studied mechanisms. We design an experiment to investigate how different information structure influence endogenous goal setting and subsequent productivity. Results show that providing information on the goals set by others encourages participants to set higher goals. Moreover, such information also promotes productivity (and payoff) via changing the endogenously set goals but the effect only pertains to the male participants. This is because that the extra information encourages both male and female participants to adjust up their goals but also significantly increases female participants' failure rate (i.e. not achieving the goal). Further analyses on the dynamics of goal setting reveal different responses to the gap between performance and goal. In the private information treatment, participants respond more to negative gaps (i.e. failure) while participants respond more to positive gaps (i.e. success) in the public information treatments.
